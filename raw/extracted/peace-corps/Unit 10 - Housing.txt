GRAMMAR EXPLANATION

1. USING MULTIPLE SUFFIXES IN ONE WORD 

	Sometimes	a	word	has	two	or	more	suffxes	in	Mongolian.	There	can	be	two	suffxes	in	one	word.

1.	The	suffx	–чих-	expresses	an	action	which	is	fully	completed.
2.	The	suffx	–лаа4	expresses	an	action	that	happened	in	the	recent	past	
(or is about to happen in the immediate future).

Эвдэр+чих+лээ = эвдэрчихлээ.

Combining	these	suffxes	has	the	meaning	of	an	action	that	happened	recently	and	is	fnished.

Би даалгавраа хийчихлээ.			
Бид хоолоо идчихлээ.  
The	usage	of	this	suffx	is	very	popular	and	it	is	pronounced	very	quickly.

I’ve	just	fnished	doing	my	homework.
We have just eaten our meal.

2. THE CAUSATIVE CASE

In most cases, the causative case means ‘to cause some action’ that is formed by the addition of the 
suffx	–уул/-үүл or –лга	according	to	vowel	harmony.		These	suffxes	are	not	exhaustive;	there	are	
many causative case endings in Mongolian that are appropriate to different situations, and imply a 
different meaning.

Please	note	that	these	suffxes	are	not	exhaustive;	there	are	many	causative	case	
ending in Mongolian that are appropriate to different situations, and imply a dif-
ferent	meaning.	For	example,	you’ve	seen	the	verb	барих, to carry or hold.  At 
Наадам, you probably heard the phrase “бөх барилдаж байна,” “the wrestlers 
are wrestling.”  This word, “барилдах,” derives from the word “барих.”  The suf-
fx/infx	“-лд-”	implies	an	action	that	is	happening	between	two	parties,	usually	in	
confict.		Roughly	translated,	we	might	say	“wrestlers	are	carrying	each	other.”		As	
another	example,	“ярих,” as you know, is translated as “to talk.”  However, “яри-
лц-ах”	is	translated	as	“to	converse.”		The	Mongolian	language	has	several	suffx-
es/infxes	like	this,	that	change	the	nature	of	the	causative	action	being	performed.		
Having	said	that,	the	suffxes	presented	in	this	lesson	are	some	of	the	more	com-
mon	causative	cases.	If	you	fnd	this	as	interesting	as	I	do,	you	might	consider	
looking at the book Mongolian Grammar by Rita Kullman and D. Tserenpil, which
